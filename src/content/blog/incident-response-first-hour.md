The first hour of an incident often determines whether the response stays controlled or becomes chaotic. The objective is not to know everything immediately. The objective is to stabilize decision-making.

## Establish command

Assign one incident lead, one technical investigation lead, and one communication owner. Avoid a room where everyone investigates, everyone updates leadership, and nobody owns the timeline.

Capture the basics:

- What triggered the incident?
- What systems, users, or data may be affected?
- What evidence has already been collected?
- What containment options exist?
- Who needs an update in the next 30 minutes?

## Preserve evidence

Do not rush into cleanup before preserving logs, alerts, suspicious files, account activity, and relevant timestamps. Containment matters, but uncontrolled changes can destroy the data needed to understand scope.

## Communicate uncertainty clearly

Executives need a current assessment, not false precision. A useful update separates confirmed facts, working hypotheses, unknowns, and next actions.

Teams that practice this structure in tabletop exercises respond faster when a real incident arrives.
